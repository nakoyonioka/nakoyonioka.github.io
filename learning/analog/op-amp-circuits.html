Module 3: Op-Amp Circuits

Practical Applications of Operational Amplifiers

Operational Amplifiers, or Op-Amps, are incredibly versatile and powerful integrated circuits (ICs) that form the basis of many complex analog systems. They are essentially high-gain differential amplifiers that, when combined with external feedback components, can perform a wide range of functions, including amplification, filtering, summing, and more.

In this module, you'll explore the ideal op-amp characteristics and build two common configurations: the non-inverting amplifier and the voltage follower (buffer).

What You'll Need:

  • A breadboard
  • A dual DC power supply (e.g., +/- 9V from two 9V batteries, or bench supply)
  • One LM741 Op-Amp IC (or a more modern equivalent like TL071/TL081 for better performance)
  • Resistors:
    • One 10kΩ
    • One 1kΩ
  • Jumper wires
  • An audio source (e.g., smartphone with a 3.5mm jack cable, or a function generator)
  • A small speaker or headphones (optional, for testing amplifier)
  • A multimeter (essential for measuring voltages)
  • (Optional: An oscilloscope to visualize signals)

Op-Amp Pinout (LM741 Example):

The LM741 is an 8-pin DIP (Dual In-line Package) IC. Its typical pinout is:

  • Pin 2: Inverting Input (-)
  • Pin 3: Non-Inverting Input (+)
  • Pin 4: V- (Negative Power Supply)
  • Pin 6: Output
  • Pin 7: V+ (Positive Power Supply)
  • Pins 1, 5, 8: Offset Null, N.C. (Not Connected) - usually unused for basic circuits.

Important: Always refer to the datasheet for the exact pinout of your specific op-amp IC.

Circuit 1: Non-Inverting Amplifier

A non-inverting amplifier amplifies the input signal without inverting its phase. The gain is determined by the feedback resistors.

The gain formula for a non-inverting amplifier is:

$$Gain (A_v) = 1 + \frac{R_f}{R_i}$$

Where $R_f$ is the feedback resistor and $R_i$ is the input resistor.

Circuit Diagram (Non-Inverting Amplifier):

  1. Connect Pin 7 (V+) of the op-amp to your positive power supply (e.g., +9V).
  2. Connect Pin 4 (V-) of the op-amp to your negative power supply (e.g., -9V).
  3. Connect Pin 3 (Non-Inverting Input) to your signal input (e.g., audio source positive).
  4. Connect the 1kΩ resistor ($R_i$) from Pin 2 (Inverting Input) to GND.
  5. Connect the 10kΩ resistor ($R_f$) from Pin 2 (Inverting Input) to Pin 6 (Output).
  6. The output is taken from Pin 6. Connect it to your speaker/headphones.

For this circuit, with $R_f = 10k\Omega$ and $R_i = 1k\Omega$, the expected gain is: $$A_v = 1 + \frac{10k\Omega}{1k\Omega} = 1 + 10 = 11$$ So, a 0.1V input signal should result in an 1.1V output signal.

Circuit 2: Voltage Follower (Buffer)

A voltage follower is a special case of a non-inverting amplifier with a gain of 1. Its primary purpose is to provide impedance matching, allowing a high-impedance source to drive a low-impedance load without significant signal loss.

Circuit Diagram (Voltage Follower):

  1. Connect Pin 7 (V+) and Pin 4 (V-) to your power supplies as before.
  2. Connect Pin 3 (Non-Inverting Input) to your signal input.
  3. Connect Pin 6 (Output) directly to Pin 2 (Inverting Input). This is direct feedback.
  4. The output is taken from Pin 6.

When you apply an input signal to this circuit, the output signal should be almost identical in voltage to the input, but it will be able to supply more current to a load.

Testing the Circuits:

For both circuits, connect your audio source to the input and a speaker/headphones to the output. Play music and observe the sound. For the non-inverting amplifier, the sound should be noticeably louder than the direct audio source. For the voltage follower, the sound volume should be similar, but it will prevent loading effects on your audio source.

If you have an oscilloscope, you can visually compare the input and output waveforms to see the amplification or buffering effect.

Troubleshooting Tips:

  • No output or distorted output?
    • Power Supply: Ensure your dual power supply is correctly connected to V+ and V- pins (Pin 7 and Pin 4). Incorrect powering is a common issue.
    • Op-Amp Pinout: Double-check the pinout of your specific op-amp IC.
    • Component Placement: Ensure all resistors and wires are firmly seated on the breadboard.
    • Input Signal: Verify your audio source is providing a signal.
  • LM741 limitations: The LM741 is an older op-amp and has limitations, especially with "rail-to-rail" operation (output can't swing all the way to the supply rails) and slew rate. If you're not getting expected performance, consider a more modern op-amp like a TL071 or TL081.

You've now worked with the incredibly versatile op-amp! Understanding these basic configurations is key to designing more complex analog circuits like active filters, integrators, differentiators, and comparators.
