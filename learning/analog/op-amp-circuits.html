Module 3: Op-Amp Circuits

Practical Applications of Operational Amplifiers with Single Supply

Operational Amplifiers, or Op-Amps, are incredibly versatile and powerful integrated circuits (ICs) that form the basis of many complex analog systems. They are essentially high-gain differential amplifiers that, when combined with external feedback components, can perform a wide range of functions, including amplification, filtering, summing, and more.

In this module, you'll explore the ideal op-amp characteristics and build a common configuration using a single power supply: the non-inverting amplifier. This approach is especially useful for battery-powered projects or systems where a negative voltage isn't readily available.

What You'll Need:

  • A breadboard
  • A single DC power supply (e.g., a 9V battery, or a bench supply set to +9V)
  • One LM358 Op-Amp IC (or other single-supply compatible op-amp like MCP600x series for rail-to-rail input/output, or TLV246x for precision).
  • Resistors:
    • Two 10kΩ resistors
    • Two additional 10kΩ resistors (for the half-supply bias)
  • Capacitors:
    • One 0.1µF (100nF) ceramic capacitor (for power supply decoupling)
    • One 10µF electrolytic capacitor (for half-supply bias stability)
  • Jumper wires
  • A multimeter (essential for measuring voltages)
  • (Optional: An oscilloscope to visualize signals)

Op-Amp Pinout (LM358 Example):

The LM358 is a dual op-amp (meaning it contains two independent op-amps) in an 8-pin DIP (Dual In-line Package) IC. For this tutorial, we'll use one of the two op-amps within the chip. Its typical pinout is:

  • Pin 1: Output A
  • Pin 2: Inverting Input A (-)
  • Pin 3: Non-Inverting Input A (+)
  • Pin 4: GND (Negative Power Supply, connected to 0V)
  • Pin 5: Non-Inverting Input B (+)
  • Pin 6: Inverting Input B (-)
  • Pin 7: Output B
  • Pin 8: VCC+ (Positive Power Supply)

Important: Always refer to the datasheet for the exact pinout of your specific op-amp IC.

Biasing the Op-Amp to Half Supply for Single Supply Operation

With a single positive power supply (e.g., +9V and 0V), op-amp inputs and outputs need to be biased to a DC voltage within the supply rails to allow signals to operate correctly. We'll set this bias at approximately half your supply voltage.

Half-Supply Bias Circuit Steps:

  1. Connect two 10kΩ resistors in series between your VCC+ (positive supply, e.g., +9V) and GND (0V).
  2. The connection point between these two resistors will provide your half-supply bias (V_bias). This point should be at approximately VCC+/2 (e.g., +4.5V if your supply is +9V).
  3. Add a 10µF electrolytic capacitor from this half-supply bias point to GND. The positive leg of the capacitor connects to V_bias, and the negative leg connects to GND. This capacitor helps stabilize the bias voltage by bypassing noise.

The op-amp's actual GND pin (Pin 4 for LM358) still connects to your system's 0V.

Circuit: Non-Inverting Voltage Amplifier (Amplifying 3.3V DC)

We'll set up a non-inverting amplifier to take your 3.3V DC input and amplify it. The gain is determined by the feedback resistors.

The gain formula for a non-inverting amplifier is:

$$Gain (A_v) = 1 + \frac{R_f}{R_i}$$

Where $R_f$ is the feedback resistor and $R_i$ is the input resistor. For this circuit, we will use $R_f = 10k\Omega$ and $R_i = 10k\Omega$, giving a gain of 2.

Circuit Diagram (Non-Inverting Amplifier - Single Supply, DC Input):

  1. Connect your 9V positive power supply to Pin 8 (VCC+) of the op-amp.
  2. Connect your 0V (system ground) to Pin 4 (GND) of the op-amp.
  3. Build the Half-Supply Bias circuit as described above. You'll have your V_bias point at ~4.5V.
  4. Connect your 3.3V DC input signal to Pin 3 (Non-Inverting Input) of the op-amp.
    Note: For this direct DC input, the op-amp's non-inverting input receives the signal directly. Its internal feedback network, with its reference to ground, effectively biases the output.
  5. Connect a 10kΩ resistor ($R_i$) from Pin 2 (Inverting Input) to 0V (system ground).
  6. Connect another 10kΩ resistor ($R_f$) from Pin 2 (Inverting Input) to Pin 1 (Output).
  7. Add a 0.1µF (100nF) ceramic capacitor between Pin 8 (VCC+) and Pin 4 (GND), placed as close as possible to the op-amp pins for power supply decoupling.

For this circuit, with $R_f = 10k\Omega$ and $R_i = 10k\Omega$, the expected gain is: $$A_v = 1 + \frac{10k\Omega}{10k\Omega} = 1 + 1 = 2$$

So, a 3.3V DC input signal should result in an approximately 6.6V DC output signal. Remember the LM358's output won't reach the full 9V rail; it will be limited to about 7V-7.5V. A 6.6V output is well within this range.

Managing Unused Op-Amp Sections:

The LM358 is a dual op-amp, meaning it contains two independent op-amp circuits within a single package. In this experiment, we are only using one of them (Op-Amp A, pins 1, 2, 3). It is crucial to properly configure any unused op-amp sections to prevent them from oscillating, drawing excessive current, or causing noise.

For the unused Op-Amp B (pins 5, 6, 7), the safest and most common practice for single-supply operation is to configure it as a unity-gain follower (buffer) with its non-inverting input tied to the half-supply bias. This ensures both inputs are held at a stable DC voltage, preventing oscillations.

Steps for Unused Op-Amp B (LM358):

  1. Connect Pin 6 (Inverting Input B) directly to Pin 7 (Output B). This sets it up as a voltage follower.
  2. Connect Pin 5 (Non-Inverting Input B) directly to your half-supply bias (V_bias, ~4.5V) point. This provides a stable DC reference for the unused op-amp.

This configuration will keep the unused op-amp stable and out of the way, without affecting the performance of the op-amp you are actively using.

Testing the Circuit:

Use your multimeter to measure the DC voltage at key points in your circuit.

  • First, measure your Half-Supply Bias voltage: Place one multimeter probe on your V_bias point (the junction of the two 10kΩ resistors) and the other on 0V (system ground). It should read approximately 4.5V.
  • Measure the input voltage: Place one multimeter probe on your 3.3V DC input signal and the other on 0V (system ground). Confirm it's around 3.3V.
  • Measure the output voltage: Place one multimeter probe on Pin 1 (Output) of the op-amp and the other on 0V (system ground). You should read approximately 6.6V. This confirms the amplification.
  • (Optional) Verify unused op-amp: Place one multimeter probe on Pin 7 (Output B) of the op-amp and the other on 0V (system ground). It should also read approximately 4.5V, confirming the unused op-amp is stably biased as a buffer.

If you have an oscilloscope, you can use it to verify the DC levels and stability of the bias and output.

Troubleshooting Tips:

  • No output or incorrect voltage?
    • Power Supply: Ensure your 9V single power supply is correctly connected to VCC+ (Pin 8) and GND (Pin 4).
    • Half-Supply Bias: Verify your half-supply bias point is at approximately half your supply voltage using a multimeter. Ensure the capacitor at this point is correctly polarized and connected.
    • Capacitor Polarity: Electrolytic capacitors are polarized! Ensure the positive leg is connected to the more positive voltage side of the circuit.
    • Op-Amp Pinout: Double-check the pinout of your specific op-amp IC (LM358 in this case).
    • Component Placement: Ensure all resistors, capacitors, and wires are firmly seated on the breadboard.
    • Input Signal: Verify your 3.3V input is present and stable.
    • Output Swing Limitations: The LM358 is not a "rail-to-rail" op-amp. Its output cannot swing all the way to the positive supply rail. With a 9V supply, the maximum output is typically around 7V to 7.5V. If your calculated output for a different gain exceeds this, it will clip.
  • Unexpected readings?
    • Grounding: Ensure all grounds are connected to a common 0V point on your breadboard.

You've now successfully built and tested a single-supply op-amp circuit that amplifies a DC signal! Understanding how to bias an op-amp for single-supply operation and setting appropriate gain is a fundamental skill for many practical electronics projects.
