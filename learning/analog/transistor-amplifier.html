Module 2: Transistor Amplifier

Single-Stage Audio Amplifier

Transistors are the fundamental building blocks of almost all modern electronics. They can act as switches (digital applications) or, more importantly for analog circuits, as amplifiers. In this module, you'll build a simple single-stage common-emitter BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor) amplifier, often used for amplifying small audio signals.

You'll learn about transistor biasing, gain, and how to amplify a small input signal into a larger output signal.

What You'll Need:

  • A breadboard
  • A DC power supply (e.g., 9V battery or bench supply)
  • One NPN BJT transistor (e.g., 2N3904 or BC547)
  • Resistors:
    • One 100kΩ (for base bias)
    • One 10kΩ (for base bias)
    • One 1kΩ (collector resistor)
    • One 220Ω (emitter resistor)
  • Capacitors:
    • Two 10µF electrolytic capacitors (input and output coupling)
  • Jumper wires
  • An audio source (e.g., smartphone with a 3.5mm jack cable, or a function generator)
  • A small speaker (e.g., 8Ω, 0.5W) or headphones
  • A multimeter (for checking DC bias points)
  • (Optional: An oscilloscope to visualize amplification)

Circuit Theory:

The common-emitter amplifier configuration provides voltage gain. The input signal is applied to the base, and the amplified output is taken from the collector. Resistors are used for biasing, which sets the transistor's operating point (Q-point) to ensure it stays in the active region for linear amplification. Capacitors are used for AC coupling to block DC voltages while allowing the AC audio signal to pass through.

Circuit Diagram:

Construct the following common-emitter amplifier circuit. Pay close attention to the transistor's pinout (Base, Collector, Emitter), which can vary by model. For 2N3904/BC547, typically Emitter-Base-Collector from left to right when flat side is facing you. Refer to its datasheet.

  1. Connect the 9V power supply to the positive and GND rails of your breadboard.
  2. Voltage Divider for Base Biasing: Connect the 100kΩ resistor from 9V to the Base of the transistor. Connect the 10kΩ resistor from the Base to GND.
  3. Emitter Resistor: Connect the 220Ω resistor from the Emitter of the transistor to GND.
  4. Collector Resistor: Connect the 1kΩ resistor from 9V to the Collector of the transistor.
  5. Input Coupling Capacitor: Connect the positive leg of a 10µF capacitor to the Base of the transistor. Connect the negative leg to your audio input source (e.g., tip of 3.5mm jack). Connect the ground of your audio source to the breadboard GND.
  6. Output Coupling Capacitor: Connect the positive leg of the other 10µF capacitor to the Collector of the transistor. Connect the negative leg to one terminal of your speaker/headphones. Connect the other terminal of the speaker/headphones to GND.

Testing the Amplifier:

Before applying an audio signal, use your multimeter to check the DC bias voltages.

  • Measure $V_B$ (Base voltage): Should be around 0.8V to 1.5V (for 9V supply).
  • Measure $V_E$ (Emitter voltage): Should be about 0.7V lower than $V_B$.
  • Measure $V_C$ (Collector voltage): Should be roughly half of your supply voltage (e.g., 4V-5V for a 9V supply). This indicates a good operating point for maximum swing.

Once DC voltages are stable, connect your audio source (e.g., smartphone) to the input capacitor and the speaker/headphones to the output capacitor. Play some music at a low volume. You should hear the amplified audio from the speaker/headphones!

Troubleshooting Tips:

  • No sound or distorted sound?
    • Check DC bias points: If $V_C$ is too close to 0V or 9V, the transistor might be saturated or cut off, leading to distortion. Adjust the base bias resistors (100kΩ and 10kΩ) slightly.
    • Transistor pinout: Ensure you've connected Base, Collector, and Emitter correctly.
    • Capacitor polarity: Electrolytic capacitors are polarized. Ensure the positive leg is towards the higher voltage side (towards the transistor).
    • Speaker connection: Ensure the speaker is correctly connected between the output capacitor and GND.
    • Audio source volume: Start with a low volume and gradually increase it. Too high an input can also cause distortion.
  • Weak sound? Check resistor values, especially the collector resistor (1kΩ) and emitter resistor (220Ω). These affect the gain.

Congratulations! You've built a working transistor amplifier. This is a significant step in understanding how active components can manipulate signals. Transistors are at the heart of almost all amplification, switching, and digital logic circuits.
