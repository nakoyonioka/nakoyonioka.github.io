Module 3: Board Layout

Routing Traces and Component Placement

With your schematic complete and footprints assigned, you're ready for the exciting part: laying out your Printed Circuit Board! PCB layout (or routing) is the process of arranging components and drawing the copper traces that connect them, based on your schematic. This is where your circuit truly takes physical form.

In this module, you'll learn how to import your netlist into KiCad's PCB editor (Pcbnew), place components, define the board outline, and route the electrical connections.

What You'll Need:

  • Your KiCad project with a completed schematic and assigned footprints
  • KiCad installed

Importing the Netlist:

The netlist is the bridge between your schematic and the PCB layout.

  1. From the KiCad Project Manager, click the "PCB Layout Editor" icon (). This will open Pcbnew.
  2. In Pcbnew, click Tools > Update PCB from Schematic... (or press F8).
  3. Ensure "Re-associate footprints by reference" and "Update PCB from schematic" are checked.
  4. Click "Update PCB". You should see a list of components and nets being added. Click "Close" after the update is complete.
  5. Your components will appear as a jumbled pile. Don't worry! These are your footprints, connected by "rat's nest" lines indicating where traces need to be routed.

Defining the Board Outline:

First, you need to define the physical shape and size of your PCB.

  1. Select the "Edge.Cuts" layer from the Layers Manager on the right side of Pcbnew.
  2. Click the "Add graphic lines" tool () on the right toolbar.
  3. Draw a rectangle or any desired shape for your board outline. Ensure the lines connect to form a closed loop. This defines the physical dimensions of your manufactured PCB.

Component Placement:

Strategic component placement is critical for good signal integrity and manufacturability.

  1. Drag components from the jumbled pile and place them within your board outline.
  2. Consider:
    • Logical flow: Place components that connect together close to each other.
    • Power and ground: Keep power delivery paths short and direct.
    • Connectors: Place connectors at the edges of the board.
    • Mechanical constraints: Account for enclosures, mounting holes, and other physical limitations.
    • Rotation: Press 'R' to rotate selected components.

Routing Traces:

Routing is drawing the copper connections between pads.

  1. Select the "F.Cu" (Front Copper) or "B.Cu" (Back Copper) layer. For simple boards, you might use only one layer, but two layers (front and back) are common.
  2. Click the "Route tracks" tool () on the right toolbar.
  3. Click on a pad (e.g., an LED pad). A "rat's nest" line will guide you to its connected pad. Draw the trace, clicking to create corners.
  4. To change layers while routing, press 'V' to place a via. A via is a plated hole that connects traces between different copper layers.
  5. Route all the connections indicated by the "rat's nest" lines. Aim for short, direct traces. Avoid sharp 90-degree bends (use 45-degree angles).

Design Rules Check (DRC):

DRC checks your layout against manufacturing constraints (e.g., minimum trace width, spacing between traces). This is crucial to ensure your board can be manufactured reliably.

  1. Click the "Perform design rules check" icon () on the top toolbar.
  2. Click "Run DRC".
  3. Review and fix any errors (e.g., "Clearance violation").

3D Viewer (Optional):

KiCad's 3D viewer lets you visualize your board before manufacturing.

  1. Click View > 3D Viewer (or press Alt+3).
  2. You can rotate and inspect your board to catch any visual issues.

You've successfully laid out your first PCB in KiCad! This is a significant achievement, transforming your schematic into a physical design. A well-routed board is not only functional but also more reliable and easier to manufacture. In the final module, you'll learn how to generate the manufacturing files (Gerbers) and prepare your design for a PCB fabrication house.
